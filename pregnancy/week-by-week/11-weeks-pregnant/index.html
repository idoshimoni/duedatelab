11 weeks pregnant: what is happening this week

Week 11 of 40 · Trimester 1

Your baby is about the size of a fig

Eleven weeks, and the end of the first trimester is genuinely close. Your baby is around 41 mm from head to bottom now, about the size of a fig.[1] It is also a practical week: tests around now may look at blood, urine, and blood pressure, so it is a good moment to check that your antenatal care is underway.[1] However uneven the symptoms still feel, use this week for orientation, not for predictions from any one body change.

What is happening at 11 weeks pregnant

The count first: eleven weeks from the first day of your last menstrual period, inside the standard 40-week convention, with conception usually about two weeks after that start.[2] So the week number rides ahead of time since fertilisation — normal, expected, nothing to recheck.

Here is the encouraging arithmetic: two weeks until the second trimester begins.[1] Hold it lightly, though — symptoms do not read the calendar, and there is no switch that flips on a specific date. Some people feel the lift early. Others still carry tiredness, nausea, headaches, dizziness, sore breasts, mood changes, or digestive complaints into the coming weeks. Both are week 11.

This week also sits right inside the first scan window. Hospitals in England usually offer two ultrasound scans during pregnancy, the first being a dating scan between 8 and 14 weeks.[1] If yours is booked, that appointment is a far better marker of progress than comparing your symptoms with anyone else’s.

And if the dates still feel confusing, remember what the label is: a clinical timing convention counted from your last period, not a statement about the exact day of conception. You can be 11 weeks pregnant without having been pregnant-from-conception for anywhere near that count.

Your baby's development this week

Forty-one millimetres, about a fig[1] — and the details this week are lovely. The fingers and toes are separating, tiny fingernails and miniature ears are in place, and the head is still large while the body grows quickly to catch up.[1] None of it can be felt from the outside; all of it is happening anyway.

The best-kept secret of week 11: your baby is already kicking around inside the womb — you just will not feel it for several weeks yet.[1] Movement inside and movement you can feel are two different milestones, and the gap between them is completely normal. A quiet week 11 says nothing at all.

That mismatch — so much on paper, so little to notice — is the signature of this stage. Development is sprinting while your body offers a mixed bag of tiredness, digestive changes, mood swings, or barely a sign. Treat the size as scale rather than a target, and let the scan speak to the specifics.

What may be happening to you

The week-11 symptom list is exhaustive, and exhausting: you may be feeling very tired, with possible aches and pains around the bump, nausea, mood swings, a metallic taste, sore breasts, indigestion, heartburn, headaches, dizziness, changed feelings about food and drink, a heightened sense of smell, milky discharge, light spotting, cramping, skin changes, shinier hair, and bloating.[1] A list that broad is really one instruction: do not over-read any single item on it.

If you have been feeling hot, sweaty, or dizzy, there is a reason: pregnancy increases blood volume by up to 50% more than usual.[1] That is body-wide context for the whole pregnancy, not a week-11 gauge you can check at home — but it does explain a lot.

The aches deserve one plain sentence too. Muscles and ligaments stretch as you start to bulge out, and pain that hurts a lot should go to a midwife or doctor as soon as possible.[1] Mild stretching sensations and pain that needs advice are different categories — keep them separate, and use the safety route below when in doubt.

One more note on the scan, since it is likely close: dating scans exist to refine the very convention this page keeps explaining. If the scan nudges your due date by a few days, nothing went wrong with your counting — the estimate simply improved. Take the new date, update your notes, and let the appointments follow it.

Practically, this is a record-keeping week. Save three dates: the first day of your last period, the date of the positive test, and any appointment or scan date. Those three lines will serve your antenatal care far better than a reconstructed diary of every symptom shift. And if you are tired or emotionally stretched, keep plans simple where you can — this part does not last forever.

Safety: when to get help in early pregnancy

When to get help in early pregnancy

Early pregnancy symptoms can be mild, variable, and hard to interpret from appearance alone. NHS guidance says vaginal bleeding in pregnancy is not always serious, but it can sometimes need urgent assessment. If you are pregnant and have light bleeding or spotting, contact your maternity unit if you have one, an early pregnancy unit if you are under 20 weeks and have access to one, or NHS 111 if you cannot reach those services. Call emergency services for bleeding with severe tummy pain, shoulder pain, faintness, dizziness, loss of consciousness, or heavy bleeding that soaks a pad soon after putting it on.

The callout is based on NHS pregnancy-bleeding guidance and stays broad because early symptoms can overlap.[3] If you have bleeding, severe pain, shoulder pain, faintness, dizziness, loss of consciousness, or heavy bleeding, use the NHS route rather than trying to decide from week timing. NHS week-11 guidance also says to see a midwife or doctor as soon as possible if stomach pain hurts a lot.[1]

A weekly symptom list can make patterns easier to understand, but it cannot separate routine discomfort from a concern that needs checking. Use the list for orientation and the safety route for decisions about worrying symptoms.

Booking your antenatal care

NHS week-11 guidance says you may be offered tests around now that look at blood, urine, and blood pressure.[1] The broader NHS antenatal guidance says the booking appointment usually sits in the 8 to 12 week window and that the first midwife appointment should happen before 10 weeks where possible.[4] If your route has not started yet, use your local booking pathway as soon as you can.

Scan timing is also near. NHS week 11 says the first scan is a dating scan between 8 and 14 weeks, and NHS antenatal guidance says an ultrasound scan is offered at 11 to 14 weeks to estimate when the baby is due.[1][4] The NHS 12-week scan guidance describes the dating scan as usually around 10 to 14 weeks and says it is used to work out how many weeks pregnant you are.[5]

If pregnancy timing is uncertain, a test remains a separate step from week-by-week reading. NHS says most pregnancy tests can be used from the first day of a missed period, and if you do not know when your next period is due, test at least 21 days after unprotected sex.[6] By 11 weeks, many readers will already be beyond that point, but the wording helps separate testing from symptom interpretation.

For appointment preparation, keep questions practical. Ask about scan timing, screening choices, what symptoms to report, and who to contact after hours. Those questions are more useful than trying to infer too much from whether week 11 feels easier or harder than expected.
