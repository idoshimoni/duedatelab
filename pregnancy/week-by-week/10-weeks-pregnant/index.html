10 weeks pregnant: what is happening this week

Week 10 of 40 · Trimester 1

Your baby is about the size of a strawberry

Ten weeks — the end of the first trimester is coming into view. Your baby, or foetus, is around 30 mm from head to bottom now,[1] and this is a week when a scan can look genuinely busy, even though personal scan results always belong with your own care team. It is a practical week too: booking care, dating-scan timing, and keeping your dates straight all start to matter. One reminder before we begin — the count still runs from the first day of your last period, not from the day of conception.

What is happening at 10 weeks pregnant

Ten counted weeks from your last period. The standard 40-week convention starts there, with conception usually about two weeks later — which is why the week count can feel ahead of the biology you expected.[2] The payoff is coordination: your appointments, scans, and due-date estimate all share one starting point.

You are nearly at the end of the first trimester.[1] People reach this point along very different paths — waiting for a first scan, arranging a booking appointment, or still getting used to the idea entirely. All of them fit inside week 10. The question that matters is not whether your symptoms match someone else’s timeline; it is whether your dates, your test result, and your care route are clear enough for the next step.

Emotionally, week 10 can be a mixed one: the pregnancy may feel more real just as the first formal checks are still ahead. The practical anchors help — booking appointments, first dating-scan timing, and folic acid through at least week 12.[1] Those move things forward far more than trying to read certainty out of bloating, tiredness, or one odd day.

And hold the dates lightly. If you ovulated later than expected, have irregular cycles, or are waiting for scan-based dating, the week can shift once better information arrives — that is the system working, not your count failing. The number gives everyone a shared framework; the fine-tuning belongs to your scan and your care team.

Your baby's development this week

About 30 mm from head to bottom now — the size of a small apricot.[1] As always, that is scale, not a target: measurements can vary by scan method and dating, and your own results belong with your care team.

Here is the detail that makes week 10 special: your baby is making jerky little movements, and those movements can be seen on a scan.[1] “Can” is doing careful work in that sentence — not everyone has a scan at this exact point, and not every scan view shows the same detail. But when it is seen, it tends to be a moment people keep.

You will not feel any of it yet — the movements are far too small for that.[1] The first felt kick is still weeks away, so an entirely quiet middle is exactly what week 10 should feel like from the inside.

The honest summary: rapid growth, visible action on a clinical scan, and nothing that one detail can turn into a conclusion. This guide holds the general timing; your care team holds your measurements and results. That division of labour is what keeps both useful.

What may be happening to you

The week-10 symptom list keeps most of the first-trimester cast: extreme tiredness, nausea, mood swings, sore breasts, indigestion, heartburn, headaches, dizziness.[1] Some people feel thoroughly pregnant by now. Others still feel mostly like themselves, plus one missed period. Both are on script.

If your digestion has opinions this week, there is a mechanism behind them: progesterone relaxes muscles in the womb and can loosen the muscles of the digestive tract along the way, which may contribute to symptoms such as heartburn.[1] Your body can feel quite different from the inside long before anything shows on the outside.

Inside, your uterus is around the size of a large orange at week 10.[1] Clothes may be starting to feel tighter — or not. Neither pattern, on its own, says anything about how the pregnancy is progressing. Keeping appointments moving matters more, and the safety routes below are there for any symptom that needs actual advice.

Try not to score yourself against the list. A day with fewer symptoms does not make a chart more informative than your own care pathway; a day with stronger ones does not tell you exactly what is happening either. Symptom lists are context — your care pathway is the instrument.

If one habit helps at week 10, it is keeping three questions separate: how far along the dates say you are, what is generally developing this week, and which symptoms need advice. The first can be estimated from your last period, the second is what a weekly guide like this one can describe, and the third belongs in the safety route below. Keep them apart and the week number stays exactly what it should be — useful.

Safety: when to get help in early pregnancy

When to get help in early pregnancy

If you are pregnant and have light bleeding or spotting, contact your maternity unit if you have one, an early pregnancy unit if you are under 20 weeks and have access to one, or NHS 111 if you cannot reach those services.[3] Call emergency services for bleeding with severe tummy pain, shoulder pain, faintness, dizziness, loss of consciousness, or heavy bleeding that soaks a pad soon after putting it on. Early pregnancy bleeding is not always serious, but it can sometimes need urgent assessment, and appearance alone is not enough to interpret what is happening.

Early symptoms can overlap, so the route is based on symptoms that need assessment rather than week timing alone. Bleeding, severe pain, shoulder pain, faintness, dizziness, loss of consciousness, or heavy bleeding should not be sorted by comparing timing or colour alone. If symptoms worry you, use your local maternity, early-pregnancy, or NHS 111 route.[3]

The same approach applies to symptoms that seem common but feel intense. If pain, vomiting, dizziness, or bleeding feels beyond what you expected, treat the concern as something to discuss with a healthcare route. A week number can orient you, but it cannot provide a personal safety decision.

Booking your antenatal care

The booking appointment is usually arranged between weeks 8 and 12.[1] The practical step is to start antenatal care as soon as possible after finding out you are pregnant, with the first midwife appointment before 10 weeks where possible.[4] If you are at week 10 and have not yet started the route, the useful next step is to contact the local service pathway rather than assume you have missed your chance.

The dating scan window is also close. The first dating scan is at 8 to 14 weeks, and an ultrasound scan is offered at 11 to 14 weeks to estimate the due date.[1][4] The 12-week scan is usually offered around 10 to 14 weeks and is used to see how many weeks pregnant you are and work out your due date.[5]

If you are still uncertain whether you are pregnant, most tests can be used from the first day of a missed period, or at least 21 days after unprotected sex if you do not know when the next period is due.[6] At week 10, many readers will already have tested, but the timing guidance keeps uncertainty separate from symptom interpretation.

It can help to keep a small set of dates together: LMP date, home-test date, appointment date, and any scan date. Bring those to the appointment rather than trying to settle dating questions from memory. Your local team can use the relevant information and explain what comes next.
