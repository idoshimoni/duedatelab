7 weeks pregnant: what is happening this week

Week 7 of 40 · Trimester 1

Your baby is about the size of a blueberry

Seven weeks, and this may be the week your body stops being subtle about it. The embryo is around 10 mm now, with the brain, face, arms, and lower limbs all developing quickly.[1] Meanwhile your blood volume is beginning a climb that will reach up to 50% more as pregnancy progresses[1] — one reason things can feel like a lot right now, not a change that is already complete. Use the week number for orientation; how strongly you feel it is not a scorecard.

What is happening at 7 weeks pregnant

The count first: seven weeks from the first day of your last menstrual period, with conception usually about two weeks after that — so the counted week stays ahead of time since fertilisation.[2] Worth remembering whenever you line the week number up against symptoms, a scan, or an appointment timeline.

Week 7 often feels like a mismatch: a lot of symptom, not a lot of visible anything. The calendar has reached 49 days, but the date count says nothing about how strongly you should feel. Some people are sick, tired, thirsty, and emotional this week; others coast through with barely a signal. Both versions are week 7. A guide like this can show you the common pattern — it cannot grade your individual baseline, and it should not try.

Your baby's development this week

Your baby is around 10 mm from head to bottom this week[1] — the size where the development details start to feel real. The brain and spinal cord are pushing ahead, the face is forming, and arm buds and lower-limb buds are appearing.[1] Concrete as all that sounds, the size is still an approximate guide for scale, not a target for a personal scan report.

The broader references agree on the shape of this stage — rapid early development, structure after structure taking form,[2] with supporting first-trimester context available too.[3] For the exact size, this page stays anchored to a single source so the numbers cannot drift.

One gentle caution: development details make tempting mirrors. A symptom pattern, a size comparison, a change in how you feel — none of it tells you how development is going. That is what scans and your care team are for, and they do it far better than any checklist.

What may be happening to you

So what might this week actually feel like? Feeling sick, tired, moody, and thirsty are all on the week-7 list.[1] Tiredness is common in pregnancy, especially in these first 12 weeks, and the same hormonal changes can leave you nauseous and emotional too.[4] Fatigue, nausea, tender breasts, and more urination round out the familiar first-trimester set.[5]

Nausea at week 7 can be genuinely frustrating, not least because it can change by the day and by the hour. It is very common in early pregnancy and usually clears by weeks 16 to 20, though some people have symptoms longer,[6] and typical onset falls between the 4th and 7th week[7] — which puts this week squarely inside the window. A common window, though, is not a required checkpoint. Missing it is not a message.

And that thirst or heaviness some people notice? Blood volume will increase by up to 50% as pregnancy progresses[1] — a pregnancy-wide change that is only beginning now, not one that is already complete at week 7. It may help explain the tiredness and the thirst. It is not something to measure yourself against.

Safety: when to get help in early pregnancy

When to get help in early pregnancy

Early pregnancy symptoms can be mild, variable, and hard to interpret from appearance alone. NHS guidance says vaginal bleeding in pregnancy is not always serious, but it can sometimes need urgent assessment. If you are pregnant and have light bleeding or spotting, contact your maternity unit if you have one, an early pregnancy unit if you are under 20 weeks and have access to one, or NHS 111 if you cannot reach those services. Call emergency services for bleeding with severe tummy pain, shoulder pain, faintness, dizziness, loss of consciousness, or heavy bleeding that soaks a pad soon after putting it on.

NHS pregnancy-bleeding guidance is the primary source for bleeding routes, while NHS ectopic-pregnancy guidance is a backup safety source for the 4th to 12th week window.[8][9] If vomiting stops you keeping food or fluids down, NHS nausea guidance also gives care-route wording.[6]

Booking your antenatal care

By week 7, most readers already have a positive test, but the NHS testing rules still matter for those with uncertain dates: most pregnancy tests can be used from the first day of a missed period, and if you do not know when the next period is due, to test at least 21 days after unprotected sex.[10] NHS also explains that tests detect hCG, which starts to be produced around 6 days after fertilisation.[10] A test gives pregnancy information; the LMP date gives dating context.

Week 7 is a sensible time to think about booking care if you have not already. NHS advises starting antenatal care as soon as possible after finding out you are pregnant, and says the first midwife appointment should happen before 10 weeks where possible.[11] The booking appointment is usually described by NHS as happening between 8 and 12 weeks.[11]

The dating scan window is later. NHS says a scan is offered at 11 to 14 weeks to estimate due date, and the 12-week scan page describes the scan as being offered around 10 to 14 weeks.[11][12] Local systems differ, so use your own maternity, GP, midwife, or local booking route for scheduling.

For week 7, it helps to avoid treating symptom strength as a performance measure. Nausea, tiredness, thirst, mood changes, and breast symptoms can all sit inside ordinary early-pregnancy variation. The sources do not say that stronger symptoms mean a better outcome or that quieter symptoms mean a worse one. If thirst is severe, paired with dizziness, or sits beside vomiting that prevents fluids staying down, use the safety and nausea-care routes rather than relying on a weekly development paragraph.
