17 weeks pregnant: what is happening this week

Week 17 of 40 · Trimester 2

Your baby is about the size of a pear

A playful comparison — every baby grows at their own pace.

By DueDateLab Editorial · May 1, 2026 · Last updated July 10, 2026 · 8 min read

Seventeen weeks, and the question everyone asks arrives: will I feel the baby move? Maybe — you might start to feel movement now, though many women first notice it between 18 and 24 weeks.[1] Your baby is around 12 cm from head to bottom, roughly the size of a pomegranate.[1] Feeling something already and feeling nothing yet are both exactly on time; here is how to hold that difference.

What is happening at 17 weeks pregnant

The dating stays put: seventeen weeks from your last period, the LMP count your due-date estimate, appointments, and scans all run on — not a direct count from conception.

This week’s headline is possibility, worded carefully: you might start to feel your baby move now,[1] and many women notice the first signs between 18 and 24 weeks.[1] Week 17 is a possibility point, not a deadline. If something flutters, lovely. If nothing does, you are inside the ordinary window, especially in a first pregnancy.

The 18-to-21-week scan window is also close now,[5] which tends to make the next stretch feel concrete. Appointment letters, scan timing, and personal results all live with your own antenatal service — and if you do feel movement, just notice the general pattern; no formal counting is asked of you yet. Between now and the scan, the checklist is short: appointment letter found, questions saved, and a note of anything new.

Your baby's development this week

Pomegranate-sized this week: around 12 cm long from head to bottom.[1] An approximate scale, not a personal target.

The skills list is getting charming. Your baby can now move their eyes (though the eyelids stay shut for now), react to loud noises, and open and close their mouth.[1] Facts to enjoy rather than a home test — reacting to sound is not the same as recognising voices on demand.

Fingernails are starting to grow, and your baby already has a set of fingerprints that belong to no one else.[1]

One number to read correctly: by the end of pregnancy, the placenta will weigh around 500 g.[1] That is where it is heading, not what it weighs now — context for how much building is still ahead across the months to come.

What may be happening to you

So, movement. You might feel it now, and many women first spot it between 18 and 24 weeks,[1] while the broader guidance says movement usually starts between 16 and 24 weeks — often after 20 weeks with a first baby.[4] If your weeks pass quietly for a while yet, you are not behind. The safety routing around movement only starts once a usual pattern has developed and then changes.

If you do feel something, it may be easy to miss: first movements often feel like a fluttering or swirling sensation in the tummy.[1] Some people catch it early; others recognise it only in hindsight. There is no requirement, and no prize for first flutter.

Meanwhile, the maybe-list rolls on: tiredness and sleeping problems, swollen and bleeding gums, pains around the bump, headaches, nosebleeds, bloating and constipation, indigestion and heartburn, sore breasts, leg cramps, feeling hot, dizziness, swollen hands and feet, urine infections, vaginal infections, darker skin, greasier skin, and thicker, shinier hair.[1] The lists overlap from week to week because symptoms refuse to move in neat weekly blocks.

A practical habit for this stretch: track questions, not certainty. Something new? Write down what it felt like, when it happened, and whether it repeated — your midwife or maternity unit can do far more with that than with a day of anxious comparison. And comparison is the real trap here: one person feels flutters now, another feels nothing until well past 20 weeks, and both fit the typical window. Movement can be emotionally huge; let it be that without making it the judge of the week. Anything that worries you goes to the safety route below — today, not after one more day of waiting to be sure.

And if the waiting is making you anxious rather than excited, say so at your next appointment. That feeling is common, it is worth voicing out loud, and midwives are good at putting the window into perspective for your specific pregnancy rather than for pregnancies in general.

Safety: when to get help in mid pregnancy

When to get help from 14 to 27 weeks

If you have bleeding or spotting, get advice from a healthcare professional: call your maternity unit if you have the number, use an early pregnancy unit if you are under 20 weeks and have access to one, or call NHS 111 if you cannot reach those services.[2] If you have a severe headache, vision problems, pain below the ribs, sudden swelling of your face, hands, or feet, vomiting, or feel very unwell, call your maternity unit if you have the number, or NHS 111 if you cannot reach it, because these symptoms can be serious and need checking.[3] If you notice your baby moving less than usual after movements have started, or you have not felt movement by 24 weeks, contact your midwife or maternity unit.[4]

The movement part of the callout is especially relevant here. Tell your midwife if you have not felt movement by 24 weeks, and call your midwife or maternity unit immediately if movement is less than usual, stops, or changes after movement has started.[4] That is different from saying no movement at week 17 is automatically concerning.

For bleeding and for symptoms that need urgent assessment at this stage, follow the bleeding and pre-eclampsia care routes.[2][3] Bleeding, severe headache, vision problems, pain below the ribs, sudden swelling, vomiting, or feeling very unwell should not be interpreted from week number alone.

Booking your antenatal care

By week 17, many readers will be looking ahead to the physical-development scan. An ultrasound scan is offered at 18 to 21 weeks to check the physical development of the baby.[5] If you have not received expected appointment information, use your local maternity or antenatal contact route.

Routine antenatal care includes checks such as blood-pressure measurement and urine testing for protein.[5] Those checks matter because some concerns are picked up through appointments rather than symptoms you can assess at home. Early signs of pre-eclampsia include high blood pressure and protein in urine, which you are unlikely to notice yourself.[3]

Keep notes that make appointments easier: dates, symptoms that concern you, questions about movements, and any letters or scan bookings. The goal is not to make a personal call from one weekly description. It is to keep your timing and care route clear.

If you are waiting for the 18 to 21 week scan, keep the appointment details and local contact route together. The scan window can be a helpful anchor, but urgent symptoms should still follow the appropriate safety route.
