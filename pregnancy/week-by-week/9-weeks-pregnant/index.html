9 weeks pregnant: what is happening this week

Week 9 of 40 · Trimester 1

Nine weeks, and hormones are running the show: hCG has been doubling every 2 or 3 days and is at its peak around this week[1] — which explains a lot about how these days can feel. Your baby is around 22 mm from head to bottom, with the face, hands, feet, internal organs, and bones all developing.[1] However loud or quiet your symptoms are, that is your version of week 9, not a grade. What is happening at 9 weeks pregnant

The count is steady as ever: nine weeks from the first day of your last menstrual period, inside the standard 40-week span, with conception usually about two weeks after that start line.[2] So the week number runs ahead of time since fertilisation — and the LMP convention stays the backbone for due-date estimates, appointment timing, and scan-window language.

Week 9 often feels like a hinge. Symptoms may be at their loudest just as booking and scan questions come into view — hormone activity, common symptoms, and fast development all share this stretch.[1] It is tempting to read meaning into intensity, in either direction. Resist it gently: how strongly you feel this week is not a measurement of anything.

If it helps, keep three threads separate. What the dates say — that comes from your last period. What is developing — that is the general story below. What your body is doing — that one is yours, and if it worries you, it has its own route through care and safety guidance, not through comparison.

Your baby's development this week

Around 22 mm from head to bottom now[1] — and the details are arriving. The face is slowly forming, the eyes are bigger and more obvious, and the hands and feet are developing, along with the major internal organs.[1] Bones are starting to form too.[1]

Notice the wording: developing, not finished.[1] The wider references support the same broad first-trimester picture,[2][3] and the size is a general scale rather than a personal target — scan measurements are read in context by the people who took them.

Clinical sources can also word this part of the trimester slightly differently from one another.[2][3] If your scan report or care note uses a term this page does not, ask your care team what it means for your pregnancy — that is a better home for the question than a search box.

What may be happening to you

The week-9 symptom list is long and honest: extreme tiredness, nausea, mood swings, a metallic taste, sore breasts, headaches, sensitivity to smells.[1] The tiredness is common especially in the first 12 weeks,[4] and fatigue, nausea, breast tenderness, and more urination all belong to the standard first-trimester picture.[5] If you recognise most of that list, this is probably not your easiest week — and it will not last forever.

The nausea may be in a difficult stretch too. It is very common in early pregnancy and usually clears by weeks 16 to 20,[6] with typical onset between the 4th and 7th week[7] — so week 9 can still sit deep in the symptom-heavy zone for many people, while others are already easing. Neither timeline is more correct.

And the reason behind much of it: hCG has been doubling every 2 or 3 days and is at its peak around week 9.[1] That is useful context for why everything feels so loud — not a lab interpretation of your individual numbers. If symptoms are severe or sudden, or come with bleeding, pain, faintness, dizziness, or signs of dehydration, use the care-route guidance below and let it outrank everything else on this page.

Safety: when to get help in early pregnancy

When to get help in early pregnancy

Early pregnancy symptoms can be mild, variable, and hard to interpret from appearance alone. NHS guidance says vaginal bleeding in pregnancy is not always serious, but it can sometimes need urgent assessment. If you are pregnant and have light bleeding or spotting, contact your maternity unit if you have one, an early pregnancy unit if you are under 20 weeks and have access to one, or NHS 111 if you cannot reach those services. Call emergency services for bleeding with severe tummy pain, shoulder pain, faintness, dizziness, loss of consciousness, or heavy bleeding that soaks a pad soon after putting it on.

NHS pregnancy-bleeding guidance is the main safety anchor for this section.[8] NHS ectopic guidance remains relevant because symptoms usually develop between the 4th and 12th weeks.[9] NHS nausea and vomiting guidance also gives routes for vomiting that prevents food or fluids staying down, very dark urine, not peeing for more than 8 hours, weakness, dizziness, fever, blood in vomit, or weight loss.[6]

Booking your antenatal care

At week 9, many readers are already past first testing. For those still arriving with uncertain dates: NHS says pregnancy tests are most reliable from the first day of a missed period, and if the next period date is unknown, to test at least 21 days after unprotected sex.[10] NHS also says hCG starts to be produced around 6 days after fertilisation.[10]

Week 9 is close to several early-care windows in the NHS source set. NHS advises starting antenatal care as soon as possible after finding out you are pregnant and says the first midwife appointment should happen before 10 weeks where possible.[12] NHS describes the booking appointment as usually happening between 8 and 12 weeks.[12]

NHS also says screening for sickle cell and thalassaemia should be offered before 10 weeks.[12] The dating scan is usually around 10 to 14 weeks, and NHS says it can work out how many weeks pregnant you are and your due date.[11] If you have not yet started booking, this is a practical point to do that.

Week 9 can feel like a point where readers want more certainty, because symptoms may be strong and care planning is becoming more concrete. The most useful framing of the week number is practical: check the LMP count, understand the general development stage, and prepare for booking and scan windows. It is not a way to judge the pregnancy from one symptom day.
