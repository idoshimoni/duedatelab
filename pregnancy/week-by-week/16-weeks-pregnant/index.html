16 weeks pregnant: what is happening this week

By DueDateLab Editorial · May 1, 2026 · Last updated May 2, 2026 · 8 min read

At 16 weeks pregnant, NHS says the baby, or foetus, is around 11.6 cm from head to bottom, about the size of an avocado.[1] NHS also describes this as a time when the baby is growing quickly and may be about to have another growth spurt.[1] For the person who is pregnant, week 16 can overlap with a midwife appointment, blood-pressure checks, urine checks, and the lead-up to the 18 to 21 week scan window. The useful frame is steady orientation, not over-reading symptoms.

What is happening at 16 weeks pregnant

Sixteen weeks pregnant is still an LMP-based week count. That count keeps due-date estimates, scans, and antenatal appointments on a shared timeline. It also means your week number is not a direct count from the day of conception.

NHS week-16 guidance is practical as well as developmental. It says you may see a midwife around now, who will weigh you and discuss how you are getting on.[1] The word “may” matters because local routes can vary, but week 16 is a reasonable point to check what care is booked and what questions you want to bring.

This is also the stage where routine checks can feel more central. NHS week 16 says blood pressure will be checked and you will need to provide a urine sample.[1] NHS antenatal guidance also says appointments can include measuring blood pressure and testing urine for protein.[5] These checks are not there because you can assess every risk at home. They are part of routine antenatal care.

Week 16 can therefore feel more practical than dramatic. The week can help you understand what may be developing and what care may be scheduled, without making it sound like a fixed checkpoint for every body or every local service.

Your baby's development this week

NHS gives the week-16 size as around 11.6 cm long from head to bottom, about the size of an avocado.[1] Use that number as a general NHS scale, not as a target to compare with an individual scan.

NHS says the baby is growing quickly and is about to have another growth spurt.[1] That is general stage context, not a promise that a specific measurement change happens on a specific day.

NHS also says the nervous system continues to develop, enabling the baby to start moving arms and legs.[1] This is development inside the womb. It does not mean felt movement is expected for everyone at week 16. NHS movement guidance says movement usually starts between 16 and 24 weeks, and first pregnancies may not feel movement until after 20 weeks.[4]

Week 16 can therefore be a transition point. Movement may be biologically active, appointment checks may be more routine, and the next scan window is getting closer. Each of those signs is separate, so one quiet sign on its own does not mean something definite.

What may be happening to you

NHS week 16 lists constipation as common in early pregnancy.[1] It also lists symptoms that may include swollen and bleeding gums, pains around the bump, headaches, nosebleeds, bloating, indigestion and heartburn, sore breasts, leg cramps, feeling hot, dizziness, swollen hands and feet, urine infections, vaginal infections, darker skin, and greasier skin.[1] Those symptoms can be frustrating, but their presence or absence is not a precise measurement of pregnancy progress.

The midwife-appointment wording can also affect how you think about symptoms. If you have questions about constipation, dizziness, swelling, discharge, bleeding, pain, or headache, an appointment is a good place to raise them. If symptoms feel urgent or severe, use the safety route instead of waiting for a routine appointment.

NHS pre-eclampsia guidance explains why routine blood-pressure and urine checks matter: early signs include high blood pressure and protein in urine, which you are unlikely to notice yourself.[3] This connects routine checks with why antenatal appointments matter.

Feeling more energetic, still feeling tired, or having a mix of both can all happen around this stage. Use the weekly information to understand the general moment, but keep symptoms that worry you in the care pathway rather than in a comparison with other pregnancies.

The appointment focus at week 16 also makes this a good place to keep language practical. Routine checks may feel repetitive, but blood pressure and urine testing are part of how antenatal care watches for concerns that may not be obvious from symptoms. Routine checks belong with antenatal care; if you have questions about readings, use your midwife, maternity unit, GP, or local NHS route.

If you feel well, this week can be a planning week. If you feel uncomfortable, it can be a question-gathering week. In both cases, the aim is to understand the general timing and keep the local care route clear.

For readers who have not yet felt movement, week 16 can be a sensitive point. The NHS movement window starts at 16 weeks but extends to 24 weeks, with first pregnancies sometimes later in that range. That wider window matters because not feeling movement yet does not mean every reader is behind.

That single distinction reduces unnecessary worry while still preserving the NHS safety route once movement has started and then changes.

Safety: when to get help in mid pregnancy

When to get help from 14 to 27 weeks

From 14 to 27 weeks, symptoms can still be hard to judge from timing alone. If you have bleeding or spotting, NHS says to get advice from a healthcare professional; call your maternity unit if you have the number, use an early pregnancy unit if you are under 20 weeks and have access to one, or call NHS 111 if you cannot reach those services. Call your maternity unit, GP, or NHS 111 if you have a severe headache, vision problems, pain below the ribs, sudden swelling of your face, hands, or feet, vomiting, or feel very unwell, because NHS says these can be serious and need checking. If you notice your baby moving less than usual after movements have started, or you have not felt movement by 24 weeks, contact your midwife or maternity unit as NHS advises.[2][3][4]

For week 16, the movement line needs particular care. NHS says movement usually starts between 16 and 24 weeks, but first pregnancies may not notice movements until after 20 weeks.[4] Once movement has started, NHS says less movement, no movement, or a changed usual pattern should be routed to a midwife or maternity unit immediately.[4] That is different from saying movement is expected for everyone at week 16.

NHS pre-eclampsia guidance says severe headache, vision problems, pain below the ribs, sudden swelling, vomiting, or feeling very unwell can be serious and need checking immediately.[3] At week 16, that wording belongs in safety routing, not in a claim that a symptom means one specific condition.

Booking your antenatal care

NHS week 16 says you may see a midwife around now, and that blood pressure and a urine sample are part of the check.[1] NHS antenatal guidance also describes a 16-week appointment and routine checks such as blood pressure and urine testing.[5] If your appointment timing differs, use your local pathway rather than comparing dates too tightly.

The next scan window is approaching. NHS antenatal guidance says an ultrasound scan is offered at 18 to 21 weeks to check the physical development of the baby.[5] At week 16, this is useful planning context. It is not a substitute for asking your midwife or maternity service about local timing.

Bring practical notes if you have them: LMP date, any scan date, questions about symptoms, and anything that changed since the last appointment. The goal is not to self-sort every symptom, but to keep useful information ready for the professionals responsible for your care.

Week 16 is also a good moment to clarify contact routes. Know whether you should call a midwife, maternity unit, GP, NHS 111, or emergency services for different situations. That awareness helps you use the right route without trying to replace it.
