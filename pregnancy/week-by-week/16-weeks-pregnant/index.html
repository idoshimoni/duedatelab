16 weeks pregnant: what is happening this week

Week 16 of 40 · Trimester 2

Your baby is about the size of an avocado

A playful comparison — every baby grows at their own pace.

Sixteen weeks. Your baby is around 11.6 cm from head to bottom, about the size of an avocado — growing quickly, and about to have another growth spurt.[1] For you, this week often has a practical shape: a midwife appointment may be near, with blood-pressure and urine checks, and the 18-to-21-week scan window is approaching.[1] Steady orientation is the aim; over-reading symptoms is the trap.

What is happening at 16 weeks pregnant

Sixteen weeks from the first day of your last period — the same LMP convention keeping your due date, scans, and antenatal appointments on one shared timeline, and still not a direct count from the day of conception.

The week has a practical rhythm to it. You may see a midwife around now, who will weigh you and talk through how you are getting on.[1] May — local routes vary. Either way, week 16 is a natural point to check what care is booked and to bring along the questions you have been collecting.

The routine checks step forward too: your blood pressure will be checked and you will provide a urine sample,[1] and antenatal appointments generally include measuring blood pressure and testing urine for protein.[5] Repetitive as they feel, these checks exist precisely because the things they watch for are not things you can assess at home. That is the system carrying the load — not you.

A useful way to prepare for the appointment: bring three lists. What has changed since last time, what you want to ask, and what is worrying you even if it feels small. Midwives work with specifics, and the small worries are often the quickest ones to settle in person — far quicker than they settle in a search box at midnight.

Your baby's development this week

Avocado week: around 11.6 cm long from head to bottom.[1] A general scale, not a target to compare with your own scan.

Growth is quick right now, with another spurt about to start.[1] And the nervous system continues to develop, enabling your baby to start moving their arms and legs.[1] Real movement, on the inside — just not necessarily anything you can feel yet.

Which brings up the week-16 question: movement is usually first felt between 16 and 24 weeks, and with a first baby often not until after 20 weeks.[4] The window opens this week; it does not come due this week. A busy baby and a quiet middle are both true for most people at 16 weeks, and one quiet sign on its own means nothing definite.

It helps to know what this stage is quietly building toward: the 18-to-21-week scan window is the next big look, and the routine checks in between keep the picture continuous. Nothing about that schedule depends on how loud or quiet your symptoms happen to be.

What may be happening to you

Constipation is common at this stage,[1] and the wider maybe-list carries on: swollen and bleeding gums, pains around the bump, headaches, nosebleeds, bloating, indigestion and heartburn, sore breasts, leg cramps, feeling hot, dizziness, swollen hands and feet, urine infections, vaginal infections, darker skin, and greasier skin.[1] Frustrating, yes. A measurement of pregnancy progress, no — in either direction.

If a midwife appointment is coming, use it well. Constipation, dizziness, swelling, discharge, headaches — the nagging-but-not-urgent questions are exactly what that conversation is for. Anything severe or urgent skips the queue and goes straight to the safety route below.

The checks have a reason worth knowing: early signs of pre-eclampsia include high blood pressure and protein in the urine — signs you are unlikely to notice yourself.[3] That is why the cuff and the sample cup keep appearing. Routine is the point, and questions about your readings go to your midwife, maternity unit, GP, or local NHS route.

Feeling more energetic, still tired, or a mix by the day — all of it happens around this stage. If you feel well, make this a planning week. If you feel uncomfortable, make it a question-gathering week. And if you have not felt movement yet, hold the wide window in mind: it runs from 16 to 24 weeks, often later in first pregnancies. Not feeling movement yet does not put you behind — the safety routing around movement only begins once a usual pattern has formed and then changes.

One last thing: comparing notes with other pregnant people can be lovely and unsettling in the same conversation. Their week 16 is not yours — different bodies, different baselines, different local routes. Borrow the tips, skip the benchmarks. Your appointments, your checks, and your own sense of how this week is going are the only three measures that matter here.

Safety: when to get help in mid pregnancy

When to get help from 14 to 27 weeks

From 14 to 27 weeks, symptoms can still be hard to judge from timing alone. If you have bleeding or spotting, NHS says to get advice from a healthcare professional; call your maternity unit if you have the number, use an early pregnancy unit if you are under 20 weeks and have access to one, or call NHS 111 if you cannot reach those services. Call your maternity unit, GP, or NHS 111 if you have a severe headache, vision problems, pain below the ribs, sudden swelling of your face, hands, or feet, vomiting, or feel very unwell, because NHS says these can be serious and need checking. If you notice your baby moving less than usual after movements have started, or you have not felt movement by 24 weeks, contact your midwife or maternity unit as NHS advises.[2][3][4]

For week 16, the movement line needs particular care. NHS says movement usually starts between 16 and 24 weeks, but first pregnancies may not notice movements until after 20 weeks.[4] Once movement has started, NHS says less movement, no movement, or a changed usual pattern should be routed to a midwife or maternity unit immediately.[4] That is different from saying movement is expected for everyone at week 16.

NHS pre-eclampsia guidance says severe headache, vision problems, pain below the ribs, sudden swelling, vomiting, or feeling very unwell can be serious and need checking immediately.[3] At week 16, that wording belongs in safety routing, not in a claim that a symptom means one specific condition.

Booking your antenatal care

NHS week 16 says you may see a midwife around now, and that blood pressure and a urine sample are part of the check.[1] NHS antenatal guidance also describes a 16-week appointment and routine checks such as blood pressure and urine testing.[5] If your appointment timing differs, use your local pathway rather than comparing dates too tightly.

The next scan window is approaching. NHS antenatal guidance says an ultrasound scan is offered at 18 to 21 weeks to check the physical development of the baby.[5] At week 16, this is useful planning context. It is not a substitute for asking your midwife or maternity service about local timing.

Bring practical notes if you have them: LMP date, any scan date, questions about symptoms, and anything that changed since the last appointment. The goal is not to self-sort every symptom, but to keep useful information ready for the professionals responsible for your care.

Week 16 is also a good moment to clarify contact routes. Know whether you should call a midwife, maternity unit, GP, NHS 111, or emergency services for different situations. That awareness helps you use the right route without trying to replace it.
