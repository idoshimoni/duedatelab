12 weeks pregnant: what is happening this week

Week 12 of 40 · Trimester 1

Your baby is about the size of a plum

A playful comparison — every baby grows at their own pace.

By DueDateLab Editorial · May 1, 2026 · Last updated July 10, 2026 · 8 min read

Twelve weeks. If you have been counting since the test came back positive, you know exactly how long that has felt. This is the week the first trimester starts to close, and for many people it is the week pregnancy begins to feel real — a scan on the calendar, a photo to keep, maybe a secret you are about to stop keeping. Your baby is about 5.4 cm from head to bottom now, the size of a plum.[1] Here is what is happening this week — for both of you.

What is happening at 12 weeks pregnant

First, the counting, because it confuses almost everyone: pregnancy is dated as 40 weeks from the first day of your last period, which means conception usually happened about two weeks after that date.[2] So week 12 is a dating milestone, not a conception one — and it is what keeps your weeks, your scans, and your due date speaking the same language.

The first trimester is coming to a close, with your baby about the size of a plum.[1] You might be starting to feel a little better. You might not — nausea, fatigue, headaches, dizziness, cramping, spotting, and skin changes do not read the calendar, and week 12 is not a switch.

It is also a week of decisions that have nothing to do with symptoms: whether to share the news, when the scan is, whether the dates line up. Enjoy those where you can. And if any symptom feels severe, unusual, or simply wrong, skip straight to the safety route below — it outranks everything else on this page.

One reassurance in advance: if a scan later shifts your dates, your original count was not a mistake. It just means your care team has better information now.

Your baby's development this week

Your baby measures about 5.4 cm from head to bottom this week[1] — plum-sized, and growing fast.

A lot has quietly come together. The internal organs and muscles have grown, and the heartbeat can now be picked up on an ultrasound scan.[1] For many parents, hearing it is the moment week 12 is remembered for. If anything on a scan report leaves you unsure, ask — the sonographer and your midwife are there for exactly those questions.

Behind the scenes, there is a good chance the placenta has taken over feeding your baby, a handover from the yolk sac that has been weeks in the making.[1] Every pregnancy runs on its own clock, so treat that as stage context rather than a fixed date.

The sex organs have formed too, though most scans this early cannot reliably tell the baby’s sex[1] — that reveal usually keeps for later, whatever the dating scan shows. The scan team can always explain what was and was not possible to see.

What may be happening to you

You may hopefully be starting to feel much better as the trimester winds down — many people do around now.[1] Or you may not, and that is just as normal: some symptoms ease gradually, some linger into later weeks, and some come and go by the day.

The week-12 list is long: tiredness, nausea, mood swings, a metallic taste, sore breasts, indigestion, heartburn, headaches, dizziness, changed feelings about food and drink, a heightened sense of smell, milky discharge, light spotting, cramping, skin changes, shinier hair, bloating, and brown patches on the face.[1] Finding yours on it can be quietly reassuring. But you know your body — anything that feels wrong belongs with your midwife, not a checklist.

About weight: most mums-to-be put on between 10 kg and 12.5 kg across the whole pregnancy, and mostly after week 20.[1] There is no week-12 target — eating well, drinking enough, and keeping your appointments matter far more right now.

If a symptom is worrying you, jot down when it started, whether it is changing, and who you have already spoken to. That small note will do more for your next appointment than any amount of pattern-matching against week guides.

Week 12 also carries a social weight, because so many people treat it as announcement week. That is a custom, not a rule — share earlier, later, or not yet, for reasons that are entirely yours.

And if symptoms are getting harder just as the trimester is supposed to be “ending,” that does not mean your count is wrong — the guidance leaves plenty of room for variation,[1] and the safety section below is there for anything that should not wait.

If you have just come from an appointment, keep three things separate in your mind: the scan date, your LMP date, and any screening choices. They are different steps with different purposes, and they do not need to merge into one milestone.

Week 12 matters. It is also one point on a much longer line — and your own care team leads everything personal.

Safety: when to get help in early pregnancy

When to get help in early pregnancy

Early pregnancy symptoms can be mild, variable, and hard to interpret from appearance alone. NHS guidance says vaginal bleeding in pregnancy is not always serious, but it can sometimes need urgent assessment. If you are pregnant and have light bleeding or spotting, contact your maternity unit if you have one, an early pregnancy unit if you are under 20 weeks and have access to one, or NHS 111 if you cannot reach those services. Call emergency services for bleeding with severe tummy pain, shoulder pain, faintness, dizziness, loss of consciousness, or heavy bleeding that soaks a pad soon after putting it on.

This safety route comes from NHS pregnancy-bleeding guidance.[3] Week 12 can be a scan-heavy stage, but scan timing does not replace urgent advice for bleeding with severe pain, shoulder pain, faintness, dizziness, loss of consciousness, or heavy bleeding. If symptoms are worrying, use the appropriate NHS or local maternity route rather than waiting to compare them with a weekly description.

The safety route also helps keep reassurance in the right place. Normal week-by-week variation is common, but severe pain, heavy bleeding, faintness, or dizziness needs a response that is not based on the calendar week alone.

Booking your antenatal care

NHS antenatal guidance says the booking appointment usually happens between 8 and 12 weeks and says the first midwife appointment should happen before 10 weeks where possible.[4] If care is already booked, week 12 may be about keeping track of appointment letters, scan timing, and any questions you want to ask. If care has not started, use the local pathway to self-refer or contact the relevant service.

The dating scan window is central here. NHS antenatal guidance says an ultrasound scan is offered at 11 to 14 weeks to estimate when the baby is due, and the NHS 12-week scan guidance says the scan is usually around 10 to 14 weeks.[4][5] NHS also says the scan may include screening for Down’s syndrome, Edwards’ syndrome, and Patau’s syndrome if you have agreed to it and if the scan happens between 10 and 14 weeks.[5]

If pregnancy is still uncertain, NHS pregnancy-test guidance says most tests can be used from the first day of a missed period, or at least 21 days after unprotected sex if you do not know when your next period is due.[6] At 12 weeks, the more common question is dates rather than whether to test, but keeping the test source separate helps avoid using symptoms as proof.

For scan and screening questions, use the appointment itself as the right place to ask. DueDateLab can explain timing, while local consent discussions and the results process belong with your care team.
