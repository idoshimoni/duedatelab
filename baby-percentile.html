Baby Percentile Calculator

Written by DueDateLab Editorial Team · Last updated May 1, 2026

WHO growth curves for weight & length, birth to 5 years.

Accepts 0 to 60 months (0 to 5 years).
Short answer: A baby percentile compares your child's weight or length to a reference population. This tool uses the WHO Child Growth Standards for ages 0 to 5 years (0 to 60 months). The 50th percentile is the median. See the methodology for the LMS formula and sources.
Share

How percentile works

Your child's percentile compares their measurement to the WHO's international growth standard. A 60th-percentile weight means 60% of babies of the same age and sex weigh less. Any value in the 3rd to 97th percentile range is considered healthy. What matters most is the trend over time, not a single number. If you are still expecting, our Due Date Calculator sets a baseline for the first measurement at birth.

When to ask your pediatrician

A single measurement below the 3rd or above the 97th percentile isn't automatically a problem, but a sudden jump or drop across percentile bands is worth mentioning at your next visit. Sleep is the other main growth signal, see our Sleep Needs by Age guide.

Sources

  • WHO Child Growth Standards: 0 to 5 years
  • WHO: Weight-for-age tables (boys & girls, LMS values)
  • WHO: Length/height-for-age tables
  • CDC: Use of WHO and CDC growth charts in the US

Percentiles are computed from WHO LMS values using a standard normal-CDF approximation. Any reading in the 3rd to 97th percentile range is considered healthy; trends matter more than single points.

Frequently asked questions

What does the 50th percentile mean?

A 50th-percentile weight means your baby weighs more than about half of babies the same age and sex, and less than the other half. Percentile is a ranking, not a grade. Any reading in the 3rd to 97th percentile range is considered healthy. Babies do not need to be at or near the 50th percentile to be growing well.

Is a low percentile a problem?

Not by itself. A consistent low percentile with a steady growth trend is usually fine, and some children are constitutionally small. What matters is the trend over time: a sudden drop across percentile bands is worth raising with your pediatrician at the next visit, especially if it coincides with feeding or illness changes.

What is the difference between WHO and CDC growth charts?

The WHO charts describe how healthy, breastfed children grow under ideal conditions, and the CDC recommends them from birth to 24 months. From 2 to 20 years the CDC charts, which describe how US children have actually grown, are used instead. The switch is standard practice and does not mean your child's growth has changed.

Does my baby's percentile change over time?

Yes, and gradual changes are normal, especially in the first two years as babies transition from birth weight patterns to their genetic growth trajectory. Persistent, steep changes across two or more percentile bands warrant a clinician's eye. Otherwise, minor fluctuations between visits are expected and reflect normal variation rather than a problem.

Which measurement matters most?

This tool reports percentiles for weight and length. Clinicians also track head circumference in the first two years and look at all three together rather than any single number. Weight shifts fastest with feeding and illness. Length reflects longer-term growth. Head circumference is used to screen for neurological development.

How often should my baby be measured?

Well-child visits in the first year typically measure at birth, 1 month, 2 months, 4 months, 6 months, 9 months, and 12 months. The second year usually adds 15, 18, and 24 months. Measurement frequency drops after age 2 to annual checks, and more often if your pediatrician wants to track a specific concern.
