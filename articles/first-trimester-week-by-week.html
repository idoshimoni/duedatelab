First Trimester Week by Week: What to Expect Through Week 13

By DueDateLab Editorial · April 21, 2026 · 8 min read

The first trimester covers weeks 1 through 13 of pregnancy, measured from the first day of your last period. It contains a fast stretch of fetal development and carries a higher miscarriage risk than later pregnancy. Many people feel their most tired during these weeks. Per ACOG, nausea and vomiting of pregnancy usually begin before 9 weeks and improve by about 14 weeks for most women.

Weeks 1 to 4: fertilization and implantation

Because due dates are measured from LMP, weeks 1 and 2 are technically before conception. Ovulation and fertilization happen around the end of week 2. The fertilized egg becomes a blastocyst and implants into the uterine lining during week 3 or early week 4. Around this time hCG production starts, which is what home pregnancy tests detect. hCG can appear in blood and urine as early as about 10 days after conception (MedlinePlus). To find your personal week count from either LMP or conception, use our Due Date Calculator.

Weeks 5 to 8: the embryonic stage

Between weeks 5 and 8 the embryo develops the neural tube, the beginnings of the heart, and the buds that become arms and legs. A heartbeat is usually detectable by transvaginal ultrasound around week 6 to 7. By week 8, major structures have started to form, and the embryo is still very small. This is the period of highest vulnerability to teratogens, which is why OBs review medications, alcohol, and smoking early. The CDC recommends 400 mcg of folic acid daily before pregnancy and during early pregnancy to help prevent neural-tube defects.

Weeks 9 to 13: fetal stage begins

From week 9 the embryo is technically a fetus. Fingers and toes separate, external genitalia start to differentiate (though not visible on ultrasound yet), and movement begins, too small to feel. The first-trimester combined screening test is usually performed between weeks 11 and 13 and 6 days and combines the nuchal translucency ultrasound with maternal blood markers. NIPT can be done from about 10 weeks. Per ACOG, cell-free DNA screening is the most sensitive and specific screening test for common fetal aneuploidies, though it remains a screening test and not a diagnostic test.

Common symptoms and why they happen

Fatigue, nausea, breast tenderness, frequent urination, and mood shifts are the most common first-trimester experiences, and all are driven by the hormonal changes that support implantation and placenta development. Per ACOG, nausea and vomiting of pregnancy usually begin before 9 weeks and improve by about 14 weeks for most women. Persistent vomiting that prevents fluid intake is called hyperemesis gravidarum and needs clinical care. Spotting is not uncommon and is not automatically a warning sign, but heavy bleeding or severe one-sided pain should be reviewed urgently.

Key tests during the first trimester

Three appointments anchor most first-trimester care. The first visit, usually in the first trimester, includes confirming the pregnancy, a full history, and baseline blood work. Per NICE NG201 and NHS antenatal guidance, booking tests commonly include blood group and rhesus D, haemoglobinopathy screening, anaemia, red-cell alloantibodies, hepatitis B, HIV, rubella susceptibility, syphilis, and urine testing. The dating ultrasound is typically offered between weeks 8 and 14 and measures CRL. Per ACOG Committee Opinion 700, if the ultrasound-based date disagrees with LMP by more than 5 to 7 days in the first trimester, the ultrasound date takes over. The first-trimester screening between weeks 11 and 13+6 combines nuchal translucency with blood markers, and NIPT from about 10 weeks can be added.

When to call your OB

Several signals warrant a call rather than waiting for the next visit. Heavy vaginal bleeding (ACOG, Bleeding During Pregnancy). Severe or one-sided pelvic pain (a possible ectopic sign). Persistent vomiting without fluid retention (ACOG, Morning Sickness FAQ). A fever of 100.4 F / 38 C or higher (MedlinePlus, Pregnancy and flu). Any sudden severe symptoms that feel different from your baseline. None of these automatically mean something is wrong, but all are reasons for same-day review rather than a wait-and-see.

Planning for the second trimester

As the first trimester ends, energy usually returns and nausea fades for most people. The anatomy scan is scheduled for weeks 18 to 22. Per ACOG's Ultrasound Exams FAQ, if the fetus is in a good position it may be possible to tell the sex at that scan. FAQ

When does morning sickness start?
Per ACOG, nausea and vomiting of pregnancy usually begin before 9 weeks and improve by about 14 weeks for most women. Persistent vomiting that prevents fluids needs clinical care.
When is the dating ultrasound?
Usually between weeks 8 and 14. A first-trimester CRL measurement can re-date the pregnancy if it disagrees with LMP by more than 5 to 7 days, per ACOG Committee Opinion 700.
When can NIPT be done?
NIPT is available from about 10 weeks. Per ACOG, cell-free DNA is the most sensitive and specific screening test for common fetal aneuploidies, though it remains a screening test, not a diagnostic test.
What is the miscarriage risk in the first trimester?
First-trimester loss risk is higher than later in pregnancy and decreases as pregnancy progresses. The exact risk depends on gestational age and personal history. Discuss your individual risk with your OB.
When should you announce a pregnancy?
There is no medical rule for when to announce a pregnancy. People choose different times based on privacy, support needs, and personal comfort.

Bottom line

The first trimester is a period of fast development and higher miscarriage risk than later pregnancy. Exact risk changes with gestational age and personal history.
