Baby Sleep Regressions by Month: What to Expect and What Helps

By DueDateLab Editorial · April 21, 2026 · 7 min read

Parents often describe temporary sleep disruptions around major developmental changes in the first two years. Timing and severity vary widely from child to child, and specific month-by-month calendars are not well supported by primary sources. What is well established is that infant sleep undergoes a genuine architectural change in early infancy, and that consistent bedtime routines improve sleep outcomes.

Sleep architecture matures in infancy

Newborn sleep consists of two broad states. Between roughly 3 and 5 months, infant sleep reorganizes toward adult-like cycles with distinct stages. Average infant sleep-cycle length is about 47 minutes in healthy term infants (Allen et al. 2012), and transitions between cycles create natural wake points. Parents often notice this around the 4-month mark as more frequent brief awakenings. This is a permanent developmental change rather than a temporary setback. What resolves over weeks is the baby's ability to settle back across the cycle transitions.

Common triggers for temporary sleep disruption

A number of normal developments can temporarily disrupt otherwise settled sleep. New motor skills like rolling, crawling, and pulling up often show up in the crib at 2 a.m. before they are reliable during the day. Separation anxiety, which commonly emerges in the second half of the first year, can make bedtime and night waking harder (HealthyChildren, Separation Anxiety). Nap transitions from three to two naps, two to one, and eventually one to none can create overtired evenings and more night waking until the new schedule settles. Illness, teething, and travel also disrupt sleep. Starting solids rarely causes night waking on its own (NHS, First solid foods). The timing and intensity of these disruptions varies widely; specific month-by-month regression schedules do not hold up consistently across children.

What actually helps

Consistency is the most reliable lever. A bedtime routine with repeated cues (bath, low light, a book, the same song) is associated with longer sleep duration, fewer night wakings, and faster sleep onset, with a dose-dependent relationship: the more consistently the routine is applied, the better the outcomes (Mindell et al. 2015). Keep the same routine and the same response to night waking during disrupted stretches, rather than switching bedtime methods. Keep the sleep environment looking the same at bedtime and overnight. Turn off screens at least 60 minutes before bedtime, especially for school-age children and teens (AAP HealthyChildren, Healthy Sleep Habits). If total 24-hour sleep drifts meaningfully below the AAP range for age, adjust nap length or bedtime. When to see a pediatrician

Raise it with your pediatrician if you see snoring, gasping, choking, or mouth breathing during sleep, consistent sleep well outside AAP-recommended ranges over several weeks, or extreme daytime sleepiness. These may point to sleep-disordered breathing or another medical cause.

FAQ

How long do sleep disruptions last?
Timing varies widely between children. The architectural change around 3 to 5 months is a permanent developmental shift rather than a temporary phase, but settled sleep typically returns over weeks. Specific month-by-month durations are not well supported by primary sources.
Is the 4-month sleep change real?
Yes. Infant sleep genuinely reorganizes between 3 and 5 months. Average sleep-cycle length is about 47 minutes in healthy term infants (Allen et al. 2012), and more transitions between cycles can mean more brief awakenings. It is a developmental change, not a setback.
What is the difference between a rough sleep stretch and a growth spurt?
There is not a sharp clinical line between a rough sleep stretch and other short-lived changes like increased hunger. What matters more is the overall pattern, total sleep, and whether there are symptoms that suggest illness or a breathing problem.
Should I change our bedtime approach during a rough stretch?
Consistent routines are better supported than frequent changes in bedtime approach. Keep the same routine and the same response to night waking. Bedtime routines have a dose-dependent effect on sleep outcomes (Mindell et al. 2015).
When should I see a pediatrician about sleep?
Raise it with your pediatrician if you see snoring, gasping, choking, or mouth breathing during sleep, consistent sleep well outside AAP-recommended ranges over several weeks, or extreme daytime sleepiness. These may point to sleep-disordered breathing or another medical cause.

Bottom line

Regressions are temporary developmental events, not training failures. Consistency, a look at total 24-hour sleep, and patience with developmental transitions are the levers most directly supported by the sources used here.
